Wire an LLM to a vector database and you have a chatbot. That part takes an afternoon. What almost nobody has actually built — because it's a different and much harder claim — is what I'd call Enterprise Intelligence: an organization's collective knowledge, its data, its documents, its policies, its past decisions, and the tacit judgment of the people who've been there longest, made queryable and actionable in real time, without the system becoming the thing that leaks what those same people were never allowed to see.

Start with why the first half of that claim matters at all. When policy discovery takes two business days instead of being instant, that's not a mild inconvenience — it's a tax on every decision that depends on it, levied every time, because each question queues behind the same handful of people who hold the answer in their heads. That doesn't scale with headcount. New hires re-solve problems the org already solved. Veterans in one department stay unaware someone three floors up solved the exact same problem last quarter. And when the person who actually knows leaves, the org doesn't just get slower — it loses the ability to answer at all, because the judgment behind the decision was never externalized anywhere a system could query it.

So you build retrieval over the organization's documents and call it solved. This is where most enterprise AI projects actually go wrong, and it's worth being precise about the mechanism, because it isn't a vague "trust" problem — it's a specific architectural one.

## Where the privilege escalation actually happens

Point a retrieval system at an organization's document stores and you've built something closer to a master key than a smarter search bar. [Permission-aware retrieval research](https://tianpan.co/blog/2026-05-04-permission-aware-retrieval-enterprise-rag-access-control) names the mechanism precisely: enterprise RAG indexes heterogeneous sources — SharePoint, Confluence, HR systems, each with its own ACL model — into a single vector store that typically has none of that granularity. Semantic similarity doesn't know who's allowed to see what; it only knows which chunks sit close together in embedding space. That's a genuine privilege-escalation vector, not a hypothetical one — the retrieval layer can surface HR or legal documents to someone who was never supposed to see them, and nobody misconfigured anything to make it happen. The architecture simply never encoded permission in the first place.

Patching this downstream makes it worse, not better. The same research is blunt about why: by the time an application-layer filter removes an unauthorized chunk, the model has already read it, and filtering at that stage is trivially defeated by a prompt injection or an implementation bug in the filter itself. [ConfusedPilot](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.04870) documents the practical version of exactly this, using Microsoft Copilot as its case study: malicious text embedded in a retrieved document can corrupt the model's output, a separate vulnerability in the retrieval caching mechanism can leak secret data, and the two chain together to propagate bad information across business functions — sales, manufacturing — that have no reason to distrust each other's outputs.

The fix converges from three independent directions, and it's the same fix each time, which is a good sign it's actually correct rather than a matter of taste. [Cerbos's production pattern](https://www.cerbos.dev/blog/access-control-for-rag-llms) has a policy decision point evaluate the user's RBAC or ABAC entitlements and generate a query-plan filter that scopes the vector search *before* it runs, so retrieved context is already restricted to what that specific user can see, before it ever reaches the prompt. [Couchbase describes](https://www.couchbase.com/blog/securing-agentic-rag-pipelines/) the identical pattern independently for agentic pipelines. And a [comprehensive review of RAG threats and defenses](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2603.21654) argues that flat RBAC isn't even the right long-term model for something this heterogeneous — attribute-based access control, filtering on tenant, department, and classification, is the stronger pattern, with relationship-based access control emerging for entitlements that don't reduce to a static role. Push authorization into the retrieval query itself, not after generation — that's the whole architectural argument, and it's why [Quellix Labs](https://quellixlabs.com/insights/permission-aware-ai-search-enterprise-rag-security) describes the failure mode so plainly: index the whole company into one vector store without that layer, and the LLM becomes a master key that bypasses folder-level permissions nobody thought to revoke.

This is the actual reason retrieval and orchestration were the smaller half of building [TDA](/projects), the enterprise assistant I built at Tata Steel — zero to production in two months, 150+ internal data sources integrated, now used by 13,000+ people daily across five group companies. 150+ sources means 150+ different pre-existing permission models, and the retrieval layer had to respect every one of them, not flatten them into a single index and hope the prompt caught what the architecture didn't. That work doesn't show up in a demo. It's most of what determines whether the thing survives contact with a compliance review.

## Why the audit trail can't be a phase-two hardening pass

Access control answers who's allowed to ask. It doesn't answer why the system said what it said — and that question arrives eventually, from an auditor, a regulator, or someone whose job depends on the answer having been right. [Decision Traces vs. Logs](https://www.elixirdata.co/blog/ai-agent-decision-traces-vs-logs-audit-trail-compliance) draws the distinction that matters: logs show a decision occurred, a decision trace shows how and why — request and session identity, the context actually retrieved, which policies were evaluated and what they produced, any escalation or human override, and the final outcome. [CX Today's reporting on audit trails](https://www.cxtoday.com/security-privacy-compliance/ai-audit-trail-regulatory-scrutiny/) gets at the harder claim without quite stating it outright: treated as a bolt-on, governance means enterprises "cannot reliably reconstruct inputs, outputs, and decision logic across models, prompts, policies, and humans-in-the-loop" after the fact — which is another way of saying it can't be retrofitted. If the system doesn't generate that evidence at each step, in real time, as part of its execution model, no amount of logging infrastructure bolted on afterward reconstructs the reasoning that connected input to output. You can capture a black box's inputs and outputs after the fact. You cannot capture logic that was never recorded to begin with.

This isn't just good discipline — it's already law where it counts. [Article 12 of the EU AI Act](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/article/12/) requires high-risk systems to *automatically* log events across their lifetime specifically to support traceability and human oversight; [Article 19](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/article/19/) separately sets a floor on how long those logs have to be kept — at least six months, longer wherever other Union or national law (including data-protection law) requires it. Automatic generation, not manual reconstruction, and a mandated retention floor on top — the regulation itself assumes a bolt-on compliance overlay doesn't hold up, which matches [PECB's observation](https://insights.pecb.com/ai-audit-trails-regulated-industries/) that the organizations getting this right treat audit-trail design as architecture from day one, not documentation added once someone asks for it.

## The harder problem: teaching the system to say "I don't know"

Access control and audit logging are both, ultimately, engineering problems with known solutions. Getting a model to abstain when it should is not — and that's worth being honest about, because it's the part most teams assume comes for free with a good enough model.

[Kadavath et al., out of Anthropic](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2207.05221), found that large models can be reasonably well-calibrated when properly elicited: a model can estimate the probability that its own generated answer is correct, and that self-assessed probability rises appropriately when relevant source material is actually present in context — which is directly useful for grounding a refusal in what was and wasn't retrieved. But [Selective Question Answering under domain shift](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2006.09462) shows the failure mode that matters most for an enterprise deployment specifically: a calibrator that behaves well in-domain degrades exactly when a real user's question drifts outside the distribution the system was tuned on — which is precisely the moment you most need it to know it doesn't know. And [research on abstention abilities](https://arxiv.org/html/2407.16221v1) found that current models frequently fail to abstain even when directly probed on questions they should refuse. Knowing when you don't know isn't a default LLM behavior. It has to be engineered — through retrieval grounding, a verifier, or fine-tuning — the same way tool orchestration or guardrails have to be engineered, not assumed.

[Semantic entropy](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38898292/), published in Nature, gives a workable version of this without needing ground-truth labels: sample multiple generations for the same query, cluster them by meaning rather than exact wording, and compute entropy over the clusters instead of the tokens. High semantic entropy is the signature of confabulation. The catch is cost — the original method needs 5–10x inference overhead because it requires multiple samples per query, which is why it hasn't shown up in every production system yet. [Semantic Entropy Probes](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.15927) is the practical fix taking shape: approximating the same signal from a single forward pass's hidden states, cheap enough to run in production instead of only in a research benchmark.

None of this is theoretical, and the incidents are worth naming because they're the same failure at increasing scale. [Air Canada's chatbot](https://www.forbes.com/sites/marisagarcia/2024/02/19/what-air-canada-lost-in-remarkable-lying-ai-chatbot-case/) told a customer he could retroactively claim a bereavement-fare discount; it was wrong, the airline argued in tribunal that it couldn't be held liable for what its own chatbot said, and lost — Canada's Civil Resolution Tribunal found the airline liable for negligent misrepresentation and ordered a refund. [New York City's MyCity chatbot](https://themarkup.org/artificial-intelligence/2024/03/29/nycs-ai-chatbot-tells-businesses-to-break-the-law) confidently told business owners that employers could keep workers' tips, that landlords could refuse Section 8 vouchers, and that stores could go fully cashless — all illegal — while telling users in the same breath that they could rely on it for professional business advice. Two years and one mayoral administration later, [it was pulled offline](https://themarkup.org/artificial-intelligence/2026/01/30/mamdani-to-kill-the-nyc-ai-chatbot-we-caught-telling-businesses-to-break-the-law) as "functionally unusable," after reportedly costing the city on the order of half a million dollars. Deloitte [delivered a report](https://fortune.com/2025/10/07/deloitte-ai-australia-government-report-hallucinations-technology-290000-refund/) to the Australian government worth roughly AUD 439,000 that an academic reviewer found contained fabricated citations and a misattributed Federal Court quote; Deloitte disclosed its Azure OpenAI use only after the fact and [refunded roughly AUD 97,000](https://www.cfodive.com/news/deloitte-refunds-60k-report-ai-errors-australian-government-accounting/803321/) — under a quarter of the contract value — once the errors became public. Every one of these is the identical failure at a different scale — a system that answers with the same confidence whether it's right or fabricating, deployed by an organization that hadn't built the thing that would have caught it before a customer, a journalist, or a regulator did.

An explicit "I don't know" only helps if it goes somewhere real. A refusal that routes to an inbox nobody's checking has the same practical effect as a hallucination, just slower and with worse optics — the user still doesn't get an answer, and now they've also learned the system is decoration. Escalation design has to name who receives it and what SLA they're held to, or it isn't governance. It's a settings toggle.

## What building TDA actually cost

I built TDA from zero to production in two months. It integrates 150+ internal data sources and is used by 13,000+ people daily across five group companies. Accounting honestly for where those two months actually went: retrieval and orchestration were roughly 40% of the effort. The other 60% was access control enforced at the retrieval boundary, audit logging built to reconstruct a decision rather than just record an event, a real answer for what happens when the system isn't sure, and an escalation path with a person actually on the other end of it.

That ratio is the finding, not a caveat on it. Every team that plans an enterprise AI project around model selection, and treats governance as a phase-two hardening pass, has effectively budgeted the 40% and left the 60% unstaffed. The Air Canada, MyCity, and Deloitte incidents aren't warnings about a hypothetical failure mode. They're what happens, specifically and repeatedly, when that 60% doesn't get built before the system reaches its first real user.
