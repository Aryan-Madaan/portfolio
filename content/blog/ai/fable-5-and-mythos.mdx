Anthropic shipped [Claude Fable 5](https://www.anthropic.com/news/claude-fable-5-mythos-5) last month — its most capable publicly released model — alongside a sibling called Claude Mythos 5 that almost nobody will ever get to use. I've spent time with the system card, the independent benchmarks, and a week of practitioner reviews, and I've pulled the pricing and retention facts that matter most for enterprise adoption into one table below instead of leaving them scattered across three paragraphs. Here's what's actually true, what's marketing, and why it matters if you're building governance around agentic AI rather than just using it.

## Fable 5 vs. Mythos 5 — same model, different leash

Both are the same underlying "Mythos-class" model. Fable 5 is the version with safety classifiers on, available to anyone with API access. Mythos 5 is the *same weights* with those classifiers stripped out, restricted to roughly 150 vetted organizations — cyberdefenders and critical-infrastructure providers — through a US-government-linked program called [Project Glasswing](https://www.anthropic.com/glasswing). Anthropic calls it the strongest cybersecurity model in the world, precisely because it's allowed to reason about offense without tripping its own guardrails. That's the "leagues apart" instinct you get from the launch post — you're right that this is a different tier, but the tier is mostly about *who's allowed to remove the leash*, not raw intelligence.

## Effort, context, and output — three dials, one shared cost model

Fable 5 drops the old "thinking budget" entirely. Reasoning is always on, and you control depth with an `effort` dial: **low** (short, latency-sensitive, non-intelligence-sensitive tasks), **medium** (a cost-saving step down when you don't need the ceiling), **high** (the default — the right floor for most intelligence-sensitive work), **xhigh** (Anthropic's own recommendation for the hardest coding and agentic work), and **max** (highest capability, no constraints — but it can overthink structured tasks and shows diminishing returns for the extra cost, so it's a last resort, not a default). For agent teams, running the orchestrator at high/xhigh while worker subagents run at low is the standard cost lever — you don't need every part of a multi-agent system paying frontier prices for effort it doesn't need.

That's the compute-allocation lever inside a single request. Context window and output cap are the same lever applied to the request's *shape*, and the reason both sit at 1M tokens in and 128K tokens out is architectural, not arbitrary. Self-attention cost scales quadratically with context length — every token has to attend to every other token in the window, so doubling context roughly quadruples the compute the attention layers burn through, and a 128K-token context needs on the order of 8x the [KV-cache](https://www.digitalocean.com/community/conceptual-articles/how-kv-caching-slashes-llm-inference-costs-at-scale) storage of a shorter baseline but on the order of 64x the attention computation ([source](https://mbrenndoerfer.com/writing/quadratic-attention-bottleneck-transformers-long-sequences)). That KV cache — the running store of every prior token's attention state — has to sit in GPU memory for the life of the request, which is why a bigger context window is a memory-engineering commitment for the provider, not a config toggle they flip for free.

Output tokens are worse, per token, than input tokens, for a different reason: generation is autoregressive. Every output token needs its own full forward pass and an updated KV cache; a thousand output tokens costs on the order of a thousand sequential forward passes, where reading a thousand input tokens costs one parallel pass ([source](https://medium.com/@akashdoifode13/token-economics-why-llm-output-tokens-cost-more-than-input-tokens-d69bac94c821)). That asymmetry — not margin-padding — is why output tokens are priced 3-5x above input tokens industry-wide, and it's a useful sanity check on the pricing table below: Fable 5, Opus 4.8, and Sonnet 5 all price output at almost exactly 5x input. Anthropic isn't setting output pricing per-tier — it's applying the same decode-cost multiplier across the whole family and moving only the input price to segment the tiers.

## The comparison that actually matters for procurement

| | Fable 5 | Opus 4.8 | Sonnet 5 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Input (per million tokens) | $10 | $5 | $3 ($2 intro pricing through 2026-08-31) |
| Output (per million tokens) | $50 | $25 | $15 ($10 intro pricing through 2026-08-31) |
| Context window | 1M tokens | 1M tokens | 1M tokens |
| Max output | 128K tokens | 128K tokens | 128K tokens |
| Zero data retention (ZDR) | Not supported — 30-day retention mandatory | Supported | Supported |

Two things jump out once you see it side by side instead of buried in prose. First, context window and output cap don't move across tiers here — all three sit at 1M/128K. That's not the industry norm: Anthropic's own Haiku 4.5 ships a 200K context and a 64K output cap specifically because it's built for latency and volume rather than depth ([source](https://www.datastudios.org/post/anthropic-claude-haiku-4-5-how-the-200k-context-window-and-64k-output-limit-shape-long-form-work-d)), and across the wider frontier market, filling an equivalent 1M-token window costs anywhere from $0.14 to $10.00 depending on the model — a 71x spread ([source](https://www.morphllm.com/llm-context-window-comparison)). Anthropic is choosing to segment Fable 5, Opus 4.8, and Sonnet 5 purely on price and effort, not on context or output ceiling — the tiering decision here is about how much compute you're willing to spend per token, not how much text the model can hold in its head.

Second, the retention row is the one that should stop a governance review, not the price. It's the only field in that table that isn't a number you can trade off against budget — it's a hard yes/no, and it only goes one way.

## Long-running workflows — the real headline, not the benchmark score

Fable 5 runs unattended for hours to days: it holds onto instructions, self-corrects, and doesn't drift. Stripe reportedly used it to push a 50-million-line Ruby migration through in a day — work a full team would've taken roughly two months to do by hand. Anthropic backs this with a "task budget" API feature: give the model a token ceiling for the whole agentic loop and it paces itself against a visible countdown instead of just hitting `max_tokens` mid-thought.

Worth being precise here, though — this isn't a Fable-5-exclusive trick. Sonnet 5 already runs adaptive thinking by default and holds up fine across long, continuous conversations; compaction (auto-summarizing context before you hit the window limit) ships across the entire 4.6+ model family, not just Fable 5. What Fable 5 actually buys you at the frontier is depth of autonomy over genuinely long horizons — multi-day unattended runs, not just multi-turn chats — plus instruction retention that doesn't degrade as the task gets messier. I'd put this next to what it took to scale Zen AI, the low-code agent platform I built at Tata Steel, to 300+ agents in nine months: the hard part was never getting one agent to work, it was getting an agent to keep working correctly for hours without a human reading every tool call. Fable 5's gap over Sonnet 5 here is real. It's a gap in degree, not a capability Sonnet 5 lacks outright.

## How effective is it, really

On SWE-bench Verified, an independent leaderboard (vals.ai) puts Fable 5 at ~95%, ahead of Opus 4.8's 88.6%. On SWE-bench Pro — a harder, more adversarial benchmark — Anthropic's own system card claims 80.3% using their own test scaffolding; that number hasn't been independently reproduced, so treat it as directional, not confirmed. Against GPT-5.5 and Gemini 3.1 Pro, Fable 5 leads on coding and long-context work; Gemini edges it slightly on GPQA Diamond (graduate-level science reasoning, 94.3 vs 91.3). Early practitioner reviews are consistent on one point: it's slow and expensive, and its edge only shows up on long, messy work — for quick single-shot tasks, it's not obviously worth the price gap over Opus 4.8.

## Security measures

Fable 5 is classified ASL-3 / CB-1 under [Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy](https://www.anthropic.com/responsible-scaling-policy) — their internal designation for a model that could meaningfully help an expert (not a novice) build cyber or bio-weapons capability. In practice, three real-time classifiers watch the model's internal activations during inference for cybersecurity, biology/chemistry, and model-distillation risk, plus a fourth check on frontier-AI-development requests. When one trips, the request is silently rerouted mid-session to Claude Opus 4.8 rather than refused outright — Anthropic reports this fires in under 5% of sessions, with a near-zero flat-refusal rate. Early on this over-triggered on benign security and life-sciences work; a retrained classifier shipped in July tightened the jailbreak-block rate to over 99% while trying to cut false positives on ordinary coding. For anyone calling this via API: the reroute isn't automatic — it's opt-in per request, and worth turning on by default, since a refusal without it just stops instead of resolving.

That "model-distillation" category isn't precautionary window dressing — [Anthropic has publicly accused competitors of running industrial-scale distillation campaigns against Claude](https://www.anthropic.com/news/detecting-and-preventing-distillation-attacks), prompting it to imagine and narrate its own internal reasoning step by step, purely to harvest chain-of-thought as training data for a rival model. That's also the real reason Fable 5's raw thinking is never exposed, even to you as the caller — you only get an optional, summarized version if you explicitly ask for it. It reads like an over-cautious API default; it's actually a direct response to a specific attack Anthropic has already documented in the wild.

## The catch for enterprises: data retention

Here's the caveat that matters most if you're evaluating this for regulated or sensitive workloads, not just side projects: **[Fable 5 and Mythos 5 don't support zero data retention](https://support.claude.com/en/articles/15425996-data-retention-practices-for-mythos-class-models).** Prompts and outputs are retained for 30 days on every platform that offers these models, and this overrides any existing zero-data-retention agreement your organization already has with Anthropic for its other models. Opus 4.8, Sonnet 4.6, and Haiku 4.5 are unaffected; this is a Fable-5-and-Mythos-specific policy, not a company-wide shift.

Don't wave this off as legal caution — it's a direct consequence of how the safety classifiers above actually have to work. Anthropic's own documentation gives the mechanism: [Best-of-N jailbreaking](https://support.claude.com/en/articles/15425996-data-retention-practices-for-mythos-class-models) — an attacker submitting hundreds of slight variations of one prompt hoping any single one slips through — can't be caught by looking at any one request in isolation. The only way to catch it is to notice that the same actor sent 200 near-identical prompts across a window of time, which requires having that window to look back across. The same logic applies to coordinated abuse — state-linked espionage or extortion campaigns where each individual request looks completely benign and only the pattern across many requests reveals the attack. A zero-data-retention architecture processes each query independently and discards it immediately; it structurally cannot run this kind of correlation, no matter how good the classifier is.

This also lines up with what Fable 5's ASL-3 tier actually requires under Anthropic's RSP: alongside the real-time classifiers, the safeguards include asynchronous, computationally-heavier review — a fast model screens first and escalates suspicious content to a stronger model for deeper analysis — plus jailbreak-detection classifiers that need to be retrained fast against newly discovered attack patterns, and honeypots (including fake model weights) to catch unauthorized access attempts spanning multiple sessions. None of that runs on a single stateless request; all of it needs a pool of retained, correlatable data to work against.

And this isn't a soft policy preference you can configure around — it's enforced at the request layer. [Anthropic's own platform documentation](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/manage-claude/api-and-data-retention) confirms the API hard-rejects requests (a `400 invalid_request_error`) from any organization whose retention configuration doesn't meet the 30-day minimum for these models. You can scope it — Anthropic lets you configure retention per workspace, so one workspace runs Fable 5 under 30-day retention while the rest of your org keeps zero retention elsewhere — but there's no way to run Fable 5 itself with zero retention, full stop.

Worth noting the retention model is graduated, not blanket: default handling elsewhere in the Claude lineup is stateless, the 30-day floor applies specifically to Covered models like Fable 5 and Mythos to support this kind of safety monitoring, and Anthropic reserves the right to hold data up to two years only for confirmed policy violations or legal holds. That's a deliberate three-tier design, not an arbitrary number someone picked — which is exactly why it isn't a settings toggle you can negotiate away. If your organization deals in the kind of data you'd never want sitting anywhere for even 30 days, that's a hard constraint on Fable 5 specifically, not something you configure around.

Worth being precise on jurisdiction too, since the two get conflated: Anthropic is a US company, so data retained during that 30-day window sits under standard US legal-discovery exposure — the same reality as using any US-headquartered vendor. That's distinct from Anthropic's separate June 2026 policy letting them proactively share consumer conversations with law enforcement without a court order — that clause explicitly excludes Enterprise, Team, and API accounts, so it doesn't touch anything we'd run through Fable 5. The retention question and the law-enforcement-sharing question are two different policies; don't merge them when explaining this internally.

## What this means from India

No Fable-5-specific restriction exists — India isn't on Anthropic's short blocked-country list (Russia, mainland China, North Korea, Iran, Cuba, Belarus). It's the opposite story: India is now Anthropic's second-largest market, revenue there has doubled in months, Anthropic opened a Bengaluru office, partnered with TCS, and Air India, Cognizant, CRED, and Razorpay are all running Claude in production. The one thing genuinely in progress, not yet live: [India-specific data residency](https://www.communicationstoday.co.in/anthropic-to-enable-data-residency-for-claude-users-in-india/), reportedly being built via AWS's Mumbai and Hyderabad regions so enterprise input/output/logs stay in-country even if compute doesn't. Worth noting this wouldn't fully resolve the retention question above either — residency is about *where* data sits, not *how long* Fable 5 keeps it; the 30-day floor applies regardless of region. That matters because India's DPDP Act rules became fully operational in November 2025, and RBI rules already require payment data to stay local — any regulated-sector deployment here has to plan around that gap until residency ships. Separately, Mythos 5 access has reportedly started extending to India-based cyberdefense and infrastructure organizations too.

## Where I land

The capability jump is real, especially for exactly the kind of unattended, hours-long agentic work I've spent the last two years building at Tata Steel — first Zen AI, then TDA, an enterprise assistant now rolled out to 76,000+ people with 13,000+ daily active users across 5 group companies. That's the workload profile Fable 5 is actually built for. But the safety layer is aggressive enough right now that it'll clip legitimate security and ML work along with the bad stuff, the retention floor is non-negotiable by design, and the India data-residency story isn't finished yet. I'd pilot Fable 5 on one genuinely long-horizon workflow, not put it on the approved list for everything — the gap between "capable" and "governable" is exactly where the 30-day retention row in that table sits, and no benchmark score changes that math.
