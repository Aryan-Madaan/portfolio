I don't think today's LLM architecture — transformers, trained by gradient descent on next-token prediction, scaled up again and again — gets us to AGI. That's not a scaling-skeptic take; scaling has worked, repeatedly, past several predicted walls, and reasoning models plus longer context windows are real progress. My problem is with three specific, well-documented properties of how these systems store, ground, and use information. All three are live, contested research areas rather than closed cases, and all three cut directly against what I think general intelligence actually requires.

The word I keep coming back to is jugaad. Not "hack" in the throwaway sense — jugaad is resourceful improvisation that produces something that actually holds up under real constraints: fixing a pump with parts scavenged off three different machines, running a factory around a part stuck in customs, building a watch brand from scratch against a state monopoly and a decade of licensing delays. It requires carrying forward, cumulatively, everything you learned from the last improvisation. It requires grounding your reasoning in what's physically, causally true, not just what's plausible on paper. And it requires holding a large, often contradictory set of constraints in your head at once without losing track of the ones that matter, especially as the problem gets long and messy. Whatever else general intelligence needs, it needs at least this much. And this is exactly where current architecture is structurally weak — not because the field hasn't gotten to it yet, but because of how these systems are built.

## Problem one: nothing you teach it in a session actually sticks

Neural networks trained by backpropagation store knowledge as overlapping, shared weights, updated by a single global loss. Ordinary gradient descent updates every weight the same way: θ_{t+1} = θ_t − η∇L(θ_t) — the new parameter vector is the old one, nudged by the gradient of whatever loss you're currently minimizing. Fine-tune a model on Task B after Task A, and that update rule has no term anywhere in it that references Task A's loss. Nothing is protected by default; whatever weights happened to encode Task A get moved however Task B's gradient happens to push them, because they're the same weights. McCloskey and Cohen documented the empirical consequence back in 1989: train a network sequentially on Task A then Task B, and performance on Task A collapses — far more severely than anything resembling human forgetting. French's 1999 review explains why the math above produces that outcome: because representations are distributed, the same weight participates in many different learned functions at once, so a gradient step that fits new data has no way to move Task B's error down without also moving weights Task A depends on.

Kirkpatrick et al.'s Elastic Weight Consolidation (2017) is the actual attempt to patch that missing term back in. Its loss function is:

L(θ) = L_B(θ) + Σᵢ (λ/2) Fᵢ (θᵢ − θ*_{A,i})²

L_B(θ) is the ordinary loss on the new task; the sum is the patch — a penalty added for every parameter i, proportional to Fᵢ (that parameter's Fisher information, roughly: how much Task A's performance depended on it) times how far θᵢ has drifted from θ*_{A,i}, its value right after Task A. λ just weights how much the old task's protection matters relative to learning the new one. This is a real, working fix in the sense that it measurably slows forgetting — but notice what it is structurally: a penalty term bolted onto the loss, not a change to the fact that θ is one shared vector serving both tasks. It approximates protection; it doesn't remove the reason protection was needed in the first place. Hadsell, Rao, Rusu and Pascanu's 2020 review names that underlying tension the "stability-plasticity dilemma": a single shared-weight, gradient-trained system can't simultaneously maximize openness to new information and resistance to disruption of old information. Improving one costs the other.

The obvious rebuttal is "sure, but scale fixes it." It doesn't, fully. Ramasesh et al. (ICLR 2022) tested this directly and found large pretrained models are more robust — forgetting decreases with scale — but it doesn't go to zero, the curve flattens with residual forgetting intact. And it isn't just a small-model artifact: surveys and benchmarks specific to billion-parameter LLMs under continual fine-tuning (the 2024 continual-learning survey, and the TRACE benchmark) confirm catastrophic forgetting is alive and well in exactly the systems we're deploying. Dohare et al.'s 2024 Nature paper adds a second, uglier finding: continual training under standard backprop doesn't just erase old knowledge, it progressively kills the network's ability to learn anything new — up to 90% of units go dead across thousands of sequential tasks, with performance degrading toward a linear model. I want to be fair to the dissent here: Sutton and coauthors argue this specific loss-of-plasticity problem isn't fundamental — their fix (periodically reinitializing dead units) extends learning substantially, and Sutton has said this particular finding "isn't inherent to deep networks." So even within this literature, not everyone agrees every facet is a hard wall.

And in-context learning — the thing that looks like an LLM "learning" mid-conversation — doesn't get you out of this. Theoretical work (von Oswald et al.) shows transformer layers can implement something that behaves like an implicit gradient step, but a 2025 paper made the crucial point explicit: that implicit update lives entirely in activations during a single forward pass. It vanishes the moment the pass ends. The actual weights never move. That's why it's bounded by the context window, doesn't accumulate across sessions, and gets described in the literature as brittle and ephemeral rather than durable learning.

I'll be precise about what this does and doesn't prove: it isn't a formal impossibility result. Orthogonal-subspace adapter methods (O-LoRA) get empirically close to eliminating interference at small cost. Test-time memory architectures (Titans) and retrieval-augmented external memory give deployed systems durable, addressable storage outside the base weights — which functionally solves a lot of continual-knowledge-accumulation problems by moving persistence outside the mechanism the forgetting literature is actually about. So the honest claim is narrower than "impossible": no currently deployed method has closed the loop within a single weight vector, the best escape routes work by redefining where persistence lives rather than fixing the trade-off inside gradient descent, and the field itself is still arguing about how much of this is fixable. That's still a real structural tax, not a rounding error.

## Problem two: it has never touched anything

The second piece is embodiment, and I want to flag upfront that it's a different kind of argument than the other two — representational and philosophical, not something with a load-bearing equation behind it. That's not a weakness to paper over; it's just an honest difference in what kind of claim this is. I think it's also the one that gets under-weighted by people arguing this from a lab rather than a factory floor. Rodney Brooks made the case in 1990, in a paper literally called "Elephants Don't Play Chess": intelligence in the wild comes from real-time sensorimotor loops with the physical world, not from symbol manipulation sitting on top of it — his subsumption architecture built genuinely competent robots without anything resembling a world model, purely from perception-action coupling. Stevan Harnad's symbol grounding problem, from the same year, gives the sharper version of why this matters: a system that only ever manipulates symbols in terms of other symbols is stuck in what he called the Chinese/Chinese-dictionary problem — look up any word and you get more words, never anything that bottoms out in the world itself. Text-only training is exactly that dictionary, all the way down.

I want to be fair to how this has aged, because it's aged in an interesting way. Brooks himself, revisiting his own argument in 2023 and 2024 after using ChatGPT, said he was genuinely unsettled — the consistency of language performance he'd have said required grounding, LLMs achieve without it. He still lands in the same place — "without grounding in physical reality I don't think a machine can understand in the same way we do" — but he's honest that the evidence surprised him, and anyone making this argument confidently today should be too.

Yann LeCun makes the current version of the case from inside the field building the actual models, and does it about as bluntly as anyone senior in AI does about anything: auto-regressive LLMs, in his words, are insufficient to reach human-level intelligence — "or even cat-level intelligence." His point is that a cat has a real, causal, physically grounded world model that lets it reason about novel physical situations it's never seen described in text, and no amount of scraping more text closes that gap, because the gap isn't a data problem, it's an architecture problem. His alternative, JEPA — learning predictive world models in latent space instead of predicting the next token or pixel — is his actual bet on what closes it. Melanie Mitchell makes a related but distinct case: our embodiment is fundamental to our intelligence, and large language models have the appearance of understanding without having experiences; her separate research on abstraction and analogy-making argues the missing piece isn't raw scale, it's the capacity to form and transfer abstractions the way embodied experience teaches you to.

Here's what keeps this from being a closed case, and I want to be as honest about it as I was about problem one. Google DeepMind's RT-2 showed web-scale vision-language pretraining transferring directly into grounded robotic action — a vision-language-action model, not a purely disembodied one — which suggests grounding might come from fusing modalities rather than requiring an actual physical body. A direct rebuttal to Harnad argues the strict embodiment requirement is too strong, that grounding can come from other causal or functional connections between representation and referent, not biological embodiment specifically. And even LeCun's own bet is exactly that — a bet. A JEPA-based system that actually beats LLMs at general reasoning doesn't exist yet; his own timeline for this is roughly a decade, not a demonstrated result today.

So the honest version: the embodiment critique is a live, serious position held by people who've spent careers on exactly this question, not a settled objection — but it's not proven either, and the strongest recent evidence is chipping specifically at the sharpest form of the claim, that physical embodiment itself, rather than just richer multimodal training, is what's required. What survives scrutiny: current LLMs, trained purely or mostly on text, have never had a causal loop with physical reality that would let them independently verify what any of their symbols refer to. Jugaad leans on exactly that grounding, because it usually starts with a physical or situational constraint no manual described in advance.

## Problem three: the more you give it, the less it can reliably use

Attention weight over n tokens is a softmax: aᵢ = exp(sᵢ) / Σⱼ exp(sⱼ), where sᵢ is how strongly the model has learned to attend to token i. By construction, Σᵢ aᵢ = 1 — however many tokens you add, the total attention "budget" doesn't grow, it just gets sliced thinner. I checked what that means concretely rather than taking anyone's word for it: give one token a fixed advantage of +2.0 over every distractor sitting at 0, and compute a₁ directly. At n = 10, a₁ = e²/(e² + 9) = 7.389/16.389 ≈ 0.451 — that token gets 45.1% of all attention. Add ninety more equally-weak distractors, n = 100, and the same token gets a₁ = e²/(e² + 99) = 7.389/106.389 ≈ 0.069 — 6.9%. Same advantage, same model, nothing retrained — a 6.5x drop in attention purely from more tokens competing for the same fixed sum. Liu et al.'s "Lost in the Middle" is the empirical face of exactly this: models use information at the start and end of a long context far better than information buried in the middle, because start/end tokens tend to hold the accumulated advantage that wins this competition. NoLiMa made it sharper — once you remove literal word overlap between the question and the answer, forcing inference instead of pattern-matching, degradation gets much worse, well before 32K tokens for several frontier models at the time. Veličković et al.'s "Softmax is not Enough" formalizes the general version: as n grows, the entropy of the attention distribution — H(a) = −Σᵢ aᵢ log(aᵢ) — is provably pushed toward its maximum, log(n), which is exactly the "spread thinner" effect the worked example shows numerically.

Here's where I have to be honest about scope, because it matters and it's exactly the kind of thing that gets smoothed over in these arguments. Veličković's result is specifically about out-of-distribution size generalization — a model tested at a sequence length larger than anything it was trained on. It is not a proof that in-distribution long-context performance is capped. Vendors train and fine-tune specifically at the lengths they advertise, and it shows: Gemini 1.5 reports over 99.7% single-needle recall out to a million tokens, and newer models report better multi-needle retention than older ones. That's real improvement, and it's evidence against "attention dilution is simply unfixable by engineering." Architectures that abandon dense softmax over every token entirely — sparse/entmax normalization, attention-sink designs, hybrid state-space/attention stacks — are a genuine structural exit from "weights must sum to 1 over all n," not just a patch bolted on top.

Where I still land skeptical: the extension of this into multi-agent, long-horizon reasoning is weaker evidence than it first looks. The Long-Horizon Task Mirage paper documents real, non-linear failure growth as agent task horizons extend, and attributes it to memory and planning failure modes in its own benchmark — it does not trace this back to attention entropy math, and treating "within-context dilution" and "across-step compounding failure" as one unified phenomenon overstates what's shown. A simpler explanation — ordinary multiplicative compounding of independent per-step error rates — fits the same data without needing the attention story at all. And the specific number sometimes quoted for degradation at a million tokens (92% down to 78%) comes from a personal blog, not a peer-reviewed benchmark, and newer vendor reports show better retention than that.

So the defensible version is narrower than "provably unfixable," and I'd rather state it that way than the stronger version: lost-in-the-middle and inference-under-low-lexical-overlap are real, replicated phenomena; softmax gives a genuine mathematical pressure toward dispersion specifically when a model is pushed past the lengths and distributions it was trained on; and that out-of-distribution limit is the part that actually holds up under scrutiny. Widening the window and training on that width is a real, working engineering move within-distribution. What it doesn't obviously buy you is reliable reasoning over a genuinely novel combination of long, messy, contradictory constraints the system has never seen assembled that way before — which is, not coincidentally, most of what jugaad actually is.

## Where that leaves it

Put the three together and you get a system that, first, doesn't accumulate experience the way a person doing real improvisation does — every session more or less starts over, patched around with RAG and fine-tuning rather than solved at the weight level; second, has never had a causal loop with physical reality that would let it independently ground what its own symbols refer to; and third, gets structurally less reliable at holding onto what matters as you hand it more of the messy, constraint-laden situation you actually need it to reason about, particularly once that situation looks different from what it trained on. Jugaad is precisely the regime where all three show up hardest: long-running, cumulative, physically grounded, and frequently novel in its exact combination of constraints. I'm not claiming any of this is a mathematically proven wall — the forgetting literature has real internal disagreement, the embodiment question is a live bet on both sides, and long-context degradation is measurably improving generation over generation within the lengths models are trained for. But "no currently deployed method eliminates this" is true of all three today, and none of them is the same as "solved by more scale." That's the gap I think has to close — architecturally, not just numerically — before "AGI" is the right word for where this is heading.
