The org chart says I report several layers below her. In our reverse-mentoring sessions, that direction flips: I'm the one explaining AI tools and trends to Tata Steel's Chief People Officer, under the company's Reverse Mentoring 2.0 program. The first session, I walked in with slides. I walked out having learned more about how senior leaders actually think than the slides taught her about AI.

I'd read *How to Win Friends and Influence People* years earlier and filed it under "sales tactics for people I'm not." The line that actually stuck, and that I only understood properly in that room, is Carnegie's observation that the deepest craving in human nature is to feel important. I'd assumed that applied to junior people wanting recognition. It applies at least as much to senior people — maybe more, since almost nobody tells a CPO she's wrong to her face anymore, and almost nobody asks her a question she doesn't already know the answer to.

## What actually changed in the room

My first instinct, walking into those sessions, was to perform expertise — here's the model, here's the architecture, here's why it matters. That instinct was wrong, and not for the reason I expected. It wasn't that she didn't understand the technical material. It was that leading with the technical material treated the session as a briefing, when what was actually valuable to her was a genuine question she could think out loud against.

The sessions that worked were the ones where I asked first: what's the actual people-problem you're trying to solve this quarter, and let AI be the answer to that question rather than the subject of the session. That's a small reordering, but it changes who's driving the conversation — and driving it is what makes someone feel like the session was worth an hour of a CPO's time, not a courtesy she extended to a junior reverse mentor.

## The part that surprised me

I expected to be nervous presenting technical material to someone that senior. I wasn't, particularly — I know the material. What actually took me by surprise was how much more I learned about simplifying complexity for a specific audience than I taught about AI itself. Watching her take an agent-orchestration concept and reframe it, in real time, as a headcount and workforce-planning question — something I'd never have framed that way — was the actual education in the room. Technical fluency was the entry ticket. It was never the point.

If I'd read *How to Win Friends* looking for a leadership hack, I'd have missed this. The actual lesson wasn't a technique. It was that expertise earns you the room, but curiosity about the other person's real problem is what earns you a second session.
