Xerxes Desai pitched JRD Tata on a watch business around 1977, while he was actually at Tata Press sorting out losses on a completely different assignment. His colleague Anil Manchanda had drawn up a shortlist of venture ideas, and watches came out on top. JRD wasn't convinced at first. Desai talked him into it anyway.

Then nothing happened for the better part of a decade. Not because the idea was wrong — because of India's industrial licensing regime, the actual bureaucratic machinery you had to clear before you were allowed to manufacture anything at scale. The joint venture with Tamil Nadu's state industrial development corporation didn't get formally incorporated until 26 July 1984. Seven years between "yes, let's do this" and "we are now legally allowed to."

During that gap, people close to the project wanted out. Minoo Mody did. By his own account, Desai considered it too. The one person who reportedly refused to let the effort slacken was JRD Tata himself — the same person who'd needed convincing in the first place.

The market they were walking into wasn't empty either. HMT, state-owned, had a de facto monopoly on watches in India — mechanical movements only, protected by import tariffs so effective that a customer might wait months for a single watch. Anyone trying to bring in a foreign watch legally was competing with an entrenched smuggling network that had built its entire business model around HMT's restrictions. Titan's bet was quartz analogue — a different technology altogether, built at a new plant in Hosur, forty kilometers from HMT's own base in Bengaluru, using a technology partnership with France Ebauches. JRD Tata inaugurated that plant on 11 March 1988.

Eleven years, more or less, from pitch to plant. The first watches actually shipped in February 1987, ahead of the plant's formal opening, and by the end of that first year Titan was already the country's leading producer of quartz analogue watches. They sold their millionth watch within two years of launch. HMT never really recovered its position once Titan showed up with automated manufacturing, a real retail model — the first "World of Titan" showroom opened in Bengaluru on 23 December 1987 — and, not incidentally, a lot of HMT's own experienced people.

Desai stayed on as managing director for the next fourteen years, and the eleven-year runway apparently didn't make him cautious afterward. In 1994 he told his engineers to build an ultra-thin watch, no further brief than that — the result, the Titan Edge, shipped in 2002 with a 1.15mm movement in a 3.5mm case, independently certified in Switzerland. He also founded Tanishq, the jewelry brand, under the same roof, before retiring in 2002.

None of this made it into a mainstream retelling until 2026, when a six-episode series called *Made in India: A Titan Story* dramatized it — Jim Sarbh as Desai, Naseeruddin Shah as JRD Tata, based on Vinay Kamath's book on the company. What I keep coming back to isn't the eventual success. It's the seven-to-eleven-year stretch in the middle, where by every external measure nothing was happening, and the people inside it had to keep deciding, repeatedly, not to declare it dead. That's a different skill than having a good idea. It's closer to what I think building anything real inside a large organization actually requires — not a moment of vision, but the willingness to keep showing up to a problem that refuses to resolve on any reasonable timeline, because the alternative is letting the delay itself become the verdict.
