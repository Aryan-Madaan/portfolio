First year of engineering college, I optimized for the wrong variable. Not effort — I put in plenty. I optimized for *belonging*: fitting in, being liked, picking the comfortable option when a harder one was on the table. It worked, in the sense that it felt good at the time. It also cost me a year I can't get back, and I only understood the price of it in hindsight.

Here's the part that took longer to learn than it should have: comfort and progress are not the same axis, and a year can feel completely full while moving you nowhere you'll care about in five years.

## What overcorrection actually looks like

The natural response to noticing this is to swing hard the other way, and I did. Years of deliberate overcompensation followed — chasing the harder option by default, treating comfort itself as a warning sign. That's not quite right either. The lesson was never "never be comfortable." It was "notice when you're choosing comfort and call it something else."

The useful version of this, in retrospect: comfort isn't the enemy. *Unexamined* comfort is. If you're picking the easy path and you know it's the easy path and you've decided that's fine for now — that's a decision. If you're picking it because it doesn't feel like a choice at all, that's the trap.

## Why this is worth writing down

I think about this most when I'm advising someone earlier in their career than me, because the pattern is common and the framing that would have helped me is simple: ask what you're actually optimizing for this year, say it out loud, and check back on it in six months. Not as a productivity hack — as a way to catch the version of yourself that's drifting without noticing.

The year wasn't wasted in the sense that it taught me this. But I'd rather have learned it without spending the year to do it, and that's the whole reason to write it down for someone else.
