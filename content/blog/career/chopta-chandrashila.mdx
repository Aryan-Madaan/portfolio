Family trip, not solo — my parents came along for this one, and it turned into the trek I'm proudest of, for reasons that have nothing to do with the summit. My mom was properly tired for most of the climb. You could watch her deciding, step by step, whether to keep going. She finished anyway. I don't think I've been prouder of her doing anything else, before or since.

The route goes through Tungnath first, the highest Shiva temple in the world at just over 12,000 feet, before the last stretch up to Chandrashila's summit past 13,000. Getting there early in the morning is the whole trick — no crowd yet, no one else's voice to compete with, just the temple and the cold and a kind of quiet that feels different from ordinary silence. It's hard to describe the vibe of that place without sounding like I'm reaching for something — but standing there with almost no one else around, at that altitude, before the day has properly started, is as peaceful as I've felt anywhere.

Past Tungnath, the last stretch up to Chandrashila opens up completely. Nanda Devi, Chaukhamba, Trishul, the Gangotri range — all visible at once if the weather actually holds, which it did for us, and the sunrise from up there is the reason people set an alarm that early in the cold.

We shared fires and food with strangers on that trek — people a decade or two older than me who told me things I wouldn't have heard anywhere else. One man walked me through how running a business is nothing like having a job. Someone else talked about marriage more bluntly than most people bother with a stranger half their age. And leeches — more of them than anyone warned us about, biting through gaiters like the fabric wasn't even there.
