Every other trip I've mentioned on this site is the same shape: go up, into thin air and silence, come back with a longer view. Brahmatal, Kedarkanta, Chopta Chandrashila — all mountains. Meghalaya breaks that pattern entirely, which is most of why I keep going back — twice now, each time chasing something different.

## Guwahati to Mawsynram

It starts in Guwahati with a few temple visits nearby, nothing that needs planning around. The real route begins the next day, heading toward Shillong via Umiam Lake, the Shillong view point, and a stop at Mawthei — none of them worth a full day, all of them worth the ten minutes off the road.

Mawsynram is the first real stop, and it's worth a homestay rather than a hotel — you end up asking locals about places that never make a map. Krem Dam has a cave attached to it that nobody advertises; you find it by asking around, not by searching for it. Krem Puri is the headline: an estimated 26 kilometers of cave system, of which you responsibly see only the entrance without a guide who actually knows the layout. Splits Rock and Syntein Road are short stops in between. Jakrem's hot springs and Mawjymbuin cave round out the day — worth doing if you're already there, not worth restructuring a trip around.

## Cherrapunji, and everything around it

The move to Cherrapunji starts early on purpose — Nonjerong has the sunrise worth losing sleep over. From there it's Mawlyngbnga, Laitlum Canyon, and Mawphlang's sacred forest, which is where the rudraksha detail comes in: the grove there — Law Kyntang — actually grows rudraksha trees, and you can buy fresh rudraksha on the way out. Most people assume that's strictly a Rishikesh thing. It isn't.

Elephant Falls is skippable if you're short on time; Seven Sisters Falls isn't. Umkhakoi Lake near Mawlyngbna was one of the better surprises across both trips — a water trek at a completely different pace from the caves and the ridgelines, quiet in a way the more famous stops aren't anymore. Mawdohnud and the Garden of Caves are worth the detour too, and Nohkalikai Falls, Lyngksiar Falls, and Prut Falls round out the approach into Cherrapunji itself.

Cherrapunji is where you actually settle in — two nights is close to the minimum. From there: Dain-Thlen Falls, the Double Decker Root Bridge (the famous one, always busier than you'd like), Rainbow Falls right next to it, Mawsmai Cave, and Wei Sawdong Falls. Kongthong, the whistling village, is its own half-day — the Khasi tradition there is naming people with a whistled tune instead of a spoken name, and hearing it in person is worth more than reading about it beforehand. The Mawryngkhang trek is the more physical add-on if you want one.

## Toward Dawki, then Jowai

Leaving Cherrapunji for Dawki, the route runs through Mawlynnong — Asia's cleanest village, and it earns the title, no lens tricks or good lighting required to believe it — and Raangthylliang, which has its own living root bridges without any of Double Decker's crowds. That's the one worth the extra time if you've already seen the famous version.

Dawki itself is about the water: boating on the Umngot river, clear enough that boats look like they're floating in mid-air in every photo, then camping at Shnongpdeng specifically — that's the stretch of river worth staying on, with no light pollution to compete with how clear it is at night.

The Jowai leg is quieter and less photographed: Syntu Ksiar, then a run of waterfalls — Tyrishi, Labon out in the East Jaintia Hills, Phe Phe, and Krangsury. None of them are famous outside the region, which is most of the appeal.

## Back to Shillong

The last stretch loops back through the Mawkdok Dympep valley viewpoint before a night in Shillong itself, which is the one city stop on the whole route. Police Bazaar is worth an evening for the street shopping and the walk more than for any single thing you'd buy, and Dylan's Cafe is worth it on food and atmosphere alone before the flight out of Guwahati the next morning.

If there's time to spare, Krem Liat Prah is worth the detour on its own — one of the longest surveyed cave systems in India — and Krem Chympe, with its own waterfall and cave combination, is a good add-on if you're already in that direction.

## What's worth carrying back

Beyond the rudraksha at Mawphlang: Shillong's markets are worth the time for Khasi shawls woven from Eri silk, bamboo and cane work, and the local wild honey, which has a completely different character from anything you'd get at home. For food, Khasi jadoh or a Naga-style chicken curry with fermented bamboo shoot is worth seeking out over anything more familiar on the menu. And if you're passing through Guwahati either way, the tea auction centre there is worth a stop — you can buy Assam tea close to source instead of off a supermarket shelf.

## Why this, instead of another Himalayan trek

Part of it is honestly just variance — I don't want every entry in this list to be the same mountain in a different state. But part of it is that the appeal of the mountains was never really about altitude. It was about being somewhere that doesn't care how smart you are or what you built this quarter. A 26-kilometer cave system does that job at least as well as a 12,000-foot ridge — arguably better, since a cave doesn't even give you a view to feel accomplished about. You just go in, trust the guide, and come out.
