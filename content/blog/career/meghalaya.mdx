Every trip I've mentioned on this site so far has been the same shape: go up, into thin air and silence, come back with a longer view. Brahmatal, Kedarkanta, Chopta Chandrashila — all mountains. Meghalaya breaks that pattern entirely, and that's most of why I wanted to go.

The rough shape of the route: Guwahati into Shillong, a night at Mawsynram (one of the wettest places on earth, which explains the caves), two nights based in Cherrapunji, then down to Dawki on the Bangladesh border, and back through Jowai to Shillong for the flight out.

## What I'm actually going to see

The living root bridges are the reason most people go, and I understand why — the Khasi and Jaintia communities have been training rubber fig tree roots across rivers for generations, so the bridge is a living organism, still growing, not a structure someone finished building. The double-decker one near Cherrapunji is the famous version; I'm more interested in finding the crowd-free ones — Raangthylliang is on the list specifically because it isn't on everyone else's.

Mawsynram has Krem Puri — a cave system that runs an estimated 26 kilometers underground, of which you can only responsibly see the entrance without a local guide. That's the part I keep coming back to: the guide isn't a tourist-experience add-on here, it's the actual difference between seeing a cave and getting lost in one.

Dawki is the other anchor — the Umngot river is clear enough that boats appear to float on air in photos, and camping at Shnongpdeng means a night with a river that clear and no light pollution to compete with it.

## Why this, instead of another Himalayan trek

Part of it is honestly just variance — I don't want every entry in this list to be the same mountain in a different state. But part of it is that the appeal of the mountains was never really about altitude. It was about being somewhere that doesn't care how smart you are or what you built this quarter. A 26-kilometer cave system does that job at least as well as a 12,000-foot ridge — arguably better, since a cave doesn't even give you a view to feel accomplished about. You just go in, trust the guide, and come out.

I'll report back on whether Mawlynnong actually deserves the "cleanest village in Asia" title, and whether the Dawki river is really as clear as the photos suggest or if that's just good lighting and a wide-angle lens.
