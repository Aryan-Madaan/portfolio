One friend from school, no group, no family — just the two of us and Himalayan Hikers guiding the route from Sankri. That's the whole shape of this trek, and it's exactly why it's stuck with me the way it has.

The trail runs through Juda Ka Talab first — a high-altitude lake surrounded by open meadows — before the climb properly starts toward base camp, which sits right across from the summit like it's daring you to stare at it all evening before you actually go up. Some of the final push was at night: cold enough to feel it in your teeth, headlamps the only light on the trail, both of us going quiet in the way you do when talking would waste breath you need for the climb.

Maggi at the summit, over 12,500 feet, the Gangotri and Yamunotri ranges stretched out in front of us with Bandarpunch and Swargarohini standing over everything else. We did push-ups up there — no real reason, just felt stupid not to, after coming that far in that cold for that view.

A two-person trek moves differently than a group one. There's no crowd to disappear into, so you end up talking the whole way up and the whole way down — actual conversation, not the kind you have to compete for airtime in. That's most of what I remember about Kedarkantha, more than the summit itself: fewer people, and somehow more said.
