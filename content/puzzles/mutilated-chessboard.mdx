Take a standard 8×8 chessboard — 64 squares, alternating black and white. Cut off two opposite corners (say, top-left and bottom-right). You're left with 62 squares.

<ChessboardDiagram />

You have 31 dominoes. Each domino covers exactly two squares that share an edge.

**Can you tile the mutilated board completely with 31 dominoes, with no overlaps and no gaps?**

You don't need to find a tiling or prove it's impossible by exhaustive search — there's a short argument either way.

<Spoiler>

No — it's impossible, and you can prove it without checking a single arrangement.

On a standard chessboard, the two squares in any pair of opposite corners are always the **same color**. (Count along the diagonal, or just picture it: corners alternate color as you go around the board, and opposite corners are two steps apart in that alternation, keeping the same color.)

So cutting off two opposite corners removes two squares of the *same* color — say, two black squares. The board started with 32 black and 32 white squares. After the cut, you have 30 black and 32 white — 62 squares total, but no longer balanced.

Every domino, wherever you place it, covers one black square and one white square, because any two adjacent squares on a chessboard are always different colors. So *n* dominoes always cover exactly *n* black squares and *n* white squares — equal numbers, no exceptions.

But we need to cover 30 black squares and 32 white squares — an unequal split. No number of dominoes can produce an unequal split, since each one contributes one of each. The tiling is therefore impossible, regardless of how cleverly you arrange them.

The elegance here is that the proof needs zero trial and error — it's a parity argument, not a search. That's usually the fastest way to show something is impossible: find an invariant (here, "dominoes always cover equal black and white") that the target configuration violates.

</Spoiler>
