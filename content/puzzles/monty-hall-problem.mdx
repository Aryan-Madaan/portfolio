Three doors. Behind one is a car, behind the other two, goats. You pick Door 1. Before opening it, the host — who knows exactly what's behind every door — opens a different door, say Door 3, revealing a goat. He then asks: **do you want to switch to Door 2, or stick with Door 1?**

Most people's gut says it's now 50/50 between the two remaining doors, so switching can't matter. That gut feeling is wrong, and the gap between the intuitive answer and the correct one is large enough that it made mathematicians write angry letters to a newspaper column in 1990.

**Should you switch, stay, or does it not matter — and what are the actual odds?**

<Spoiler>

**Switch.** Switching wins the car **2/3** of the time. Staying wins only **1/3** of the time. It isn't close, and it isn't 50/50.

Here's the reasoning that holds up, not the gut instinct that doesn't: when you first pick a door, you're picking blind, so you have exactly a 1/3 chance of having picked the car and a 2/3 chance the car is behind one of the *other two* doors. That 2/3 doesn't move just because the host does something next — it's locked in at the moment you chose.

Now the host acts, and this is the part that's easy to underweight: he isn't opening a random door. He knows where the car is, and he will *always* open a door that (a) you didn't pick and (b) has a goat behind it. He can always do this, because at least one of the two doors you didn't pick has a goat. His reveal carries no new information about *your* door — but it dumps all the accumulated probability of "the car's behind one of the other two" onto whichever single door he leaves closed.

Concretely: your original 2/3 chance that the car is behind "one of the other two doors" doesn't vanish when one of those two turns out to be a goat — it consolidates entirely onto the *other* one, which the host deliberately left unopened. Switching means you're effectively betting on "the car is behind one of the doors I didn't originally pick," which was always the 2/3 bet — you just get to make it concrete after the host filters out one wrong option for you, for free.

If you don't trust the argument, trust the exhaustive case check — there are only three possible car locations, and the host's move is fully determined in each:

| Car is behind | You picked Door 1 | Host must open | Stay wins? | Switch wins? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Door 1 | Door 1 | Door 2 or 3 (goat) | Yes | No |
| Door 2 | Door 1 | Door 3 (forced — only goat left) | No | Yes |
| Door 3 | Door 1 | Door 2 (forced — only goat left) | No | Yes |

Staying wins in exactly 1 of the 3 equally likely car placements. Switching wins in the other 2. That's the whole proof — no calculus, just an honest table with all three cases written down, which is usually enough to end the argument the letters column couldn't.

</Spoiler>
