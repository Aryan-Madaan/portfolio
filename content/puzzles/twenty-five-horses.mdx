You have 25 horses and want the **three fastest**, in order. There's no stopwatch — the only way to compare horses is to race them — and the track holds exactly **five at a time**, giving you their finishing order for that heat. Assume no ties and that each horse runs at a consistent speed every race.

**What is the fewest number of races that guarantees you the top three, and how do you do it?**

<Spoiler>

**Seven races.**

**Races 1–5.** Split the 25 horses into five groups of five and race each group once. You now know the full ranking *within* each group — but nothing across groups yet.

**Race 6.** Race the five group-winners against each other. Whoever wins race 6 is the fastest horse of all 25. Relabel the groups **A, B, C, D, E** in the order their winners finished race 6 — so A's winner came first, E's winner came last.

**Now eliminate ruthlessly.** Two whole groups are already out: any horse in group D or E is beaten by at least three horses (the winners of A, B, C all finished ahead of D's and E's winners, who are themselves the fastest in their own groups). That's 10 horses gone in a single race. And A's winner is confirmed as the overall #1 — set it aside.

**Who can still be 2nd or 3rd?** Only five horses survive the cut:

| Candidate | Why it's still alive |
|---|---|
| A2, A3 | 2nd and 3rd in group A; only A1 is proven faster |
| B1, B2 | B's winner could be 2nd overall; its runner-up could be 3rd |
| C1 | C's winner could sneak in at 3rd |

Everyone else is provably behind at least three known-faster horses. (B3 sits behind B1 and B2, and B1 sits behind A1 — three faster. C2 sits behind C1, behind B1, behind A1 — three faster. And so on.)

**Race 7.** Race those five survivors — A2, A3, B1, B2, C1. The top two finishers are the overall **2nd and 3rd**.

Total: 5 + 1 + 1 = **7 races.**

**The lesson.** The whole trick is refusing to do work you don't need. A full ranking of 25 horses would take far more races — but you were only ever asked for the top three, so most comparisons are wasted. Race 6 is the move that pays: one heat eliminates ten horses at once by *transitivity*, not by racing them directly. Then you sort only the tiny frontier that's genuinely still in contention. It's the same instinct behind a partial sort or a well-seeded tournament bracket — spend your comparisons only where the answer is still undecided, and let logic delete the rest for free.

</Spoiler>
